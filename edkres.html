It's All About Healthcare

I want to return to the health care field in hope of reclaiming the feeling I got when I was doing pharacokinetic modeling, when I knew my model had helped to save a life.

Having been an independent contractor for many years I am getting tired of being entirely on my own. I would like to get the benefits of being a full time employee and be part of a team.

My career started in health care, first doing pharmacokinatic modeling and Emergency Room Triage for Ceder systems Ltd., then in electronic health records for the U.S. Army I learned anatomy & physiology as part of my study of massage and martial arts and I have been taking online classes in health informatics, bioinformatics, biostatistics, molecular biology, neuroscience and medical imaging to prepare myself to reenter the field. I am learning the science and the vocabulary so I can better understand requirements.

Much of my experience outside of health care I believer to be transferable. Geo-Centers' preliminary research suggested that the code I developed to locate land mines in infrared images would also be able to detect cancer tumors in x-rays. The tools I used to target direct marketing mailings to potential costumers should also be usable in a clinical decision support system. My experience with business and financial data should be directly transferable to health records.

Web, mobil & cloud based systems look like the future. I am on top af all three. I built my first web site in 1995 and have stayed on top of the changing technology. On the client side I am conferable using HTML5, CSS3, jQuery, & Bootstrap. On the server side I have used Php, Ruby, Python, node.js, and C++. I have developed Android apps and have taken courses on iOS development. I have also studied the theoretical issues of cloud computing and have experience with Heroku, AWS, Azure & the Google Cloud Platform.

Qualfications

Pharmacokinetic Modeling and Clinical Decision Support System

Rewrote & enhanced a pharmacokinetic modeling system at CSL. Assisted pharmacologists building pharmacokinetic models for treatment, research, and forensics. On at least two occasions my models were instrumental in saving lives. Created models for expert witness testimony in drunk driving cases.

Electronic Health Records

Provided deployment, maintenance, support & training for the US Army’s Occupational Health Management Information System (OHMIS) that collected electronic medical records and environmental heath exposure data world wide and consolidated it to a central data repository.

Requirements Analysis

Wrote requirements specification for FEMA's accounting system. Facilitated meetings of diverse stake holders, interviewed users, and searched legal documents to collect functional, workflow, & regulatory requirements and build a data model.

Data Modeling

Developed & supported an enterprise data modeling tool and developed teaching materials for data modeling training seminars at IESC.

Built data model to capture operational, performance, & vibration data to predict component failure & optimize maintenance schedule for Sikorsky's lift fan used in Boeing's joint strike fighter proposal while at B.F. Goodrich.

Designed Geo-Centers' proposed international standard file format for interchange of ground penetrating focused array radar data. Each group used a proprietary format that implicitly assumed the unique array geometry and timing sequences of their system. It was necessary to develop a format which could explicitly encode any posable geometric and timing configuration.

Web Based Interface To Legacy System

Developed a web based interface to a legacy retirement plan management system for TIAA-CREF. The system walked the user through a dynamic guided interview process that adapted to accommodate the answers given. The data was checked for inconstancies, and a virtual card deck was created and sent to the legacy system.

Expert Systems

Created expert systems to automate emergency room triage for CLS, evaluate data models for IESC, and guide web based interviews for TIAA-CREF.

Image Analysis & Feature Detection

Responsible for hardware & software design for and coding of the infrared subsystem of Geo-Centers' vehicle mounted multi-sensor land mine detector. Used statistical analysis, fuzzy logic, & digital signal processing to analyze 3 dimensional radar data, 2 dimensional IR images, and 1 dimensional magnetometer data to identify and locate land mines. Developed algorithm that greatly improved the resolution from course grained magnetometer data. Geo-centers considered migrating the technology for tumor detection, but decided not to enter the medical field.

Biography

I wrote my first program, hand assembled it, and typed it in hex code into a prototype GE PC code named "Watergate" when I was in Jr. High School. Since them I have programmed everything from embedded systems to super computers. I have always been fascinated by the concept of computing, including biocomputing, neuralcomputing and DNA computing.

There was an essay in the Communications of the ACM in the early 1990's which said the great thing about being a software developer was that you could change careers with little or no retraining. Don't like working at a bank, go work for a manufacturing firm; don't like working at a law firm, go work at a research lab, etc. This was very true for me. At that time I was working at the SEC, processing document related to mergers, accusations, and issuing of stock. Previously I have been a business consultant (I will never forget the day the head of a government agency called in my team and told us, "Gentleman, we have a problem. OMB wants to know what I do. I need you to figure it out." Made me wonder whether I was part of the problem, or part of the solution.) Before that I worked in health care (The feeling I got when I realised that a pharmacokinetic model I made had helped to save a life, I knew I was part of the solution), and before that I was in law publishing. Since then I have worked in satellite communication, accounting, marketing, banking, aerospace, and ordinance disposal.

I have developed software for mathematical modeling, data storage & retrieval, expert systems, enterprise data modeling, inventory control & accountable property management, time billing, satellite communications, financial reporting, accounting, data mining, automatic target recognition, Geo positioning systems, sensor integration, international funds transfer, and web based interfaces to legacy systems.

I have a BA in Math & Philosophy and have studied a wide variety of subjects ranging from history of architecture through Traditional Chinese Medicine to molecular biology & neuroscience. While I enjoy intellectual pursuits, I also keep physically active. I love the outdoors (I am an Eagle Scout) and have black belts from two different international aikido associations (The International Ki Society & Kokyukai). I have produced award winning videos, and taught candle making, C programming, martial arts, object oriented design, massage, computer repair, and stress reduction.

Languages

I wrote this blog post about the programming languages I had as of 2007. All experiences detailed there are still true (except that my opinion of Java has improved). Here is an updated synopsis of my experience with less commentary about the languages themselves.

C & C++

C and C++ are very different languages, but my experiences with the two are interlocked.

I first used C At CSL in 1985, when I ported the pharmacokinetics modeling tool from Pascal to C. Five years later IESC brought in a tutor to teach the programming team C++. In the end it was decided that the C++ tools available at that time were not up to the job, but we did use object-oriented design principals despite the fact we implemented the system in C. I started using C++ for my own programs at that time.

I fist used C++ commercially for ComSat. Where wrote object-oriented C++. The at the SEC I used C++ as a type safe C, writing code that was required to compile cleaning as either C or C++.

At Epsilon and Geo-Centers I wrote everything in C++. The web service I developed for Sungard was also written in C++, using a proprietary web framework developed in house.

Python

Python is still my goto language for most tasks. It is very easy to use and has lots of good libraries and frameworks.

Java

For years I avoided Java, seeing it as a crippled dialect of C++, and only used it when required to do so (most notably by MeetingMaker & RulesPower). Since I have been using it on my own to develop Android apps I have started to appreciated it as a language in it's own right with its own strengths and weaknesses. While it is true that Java is not C++, I now see that C++ is not Java. While there are many tasks for which C++ is better suited than Java, there are others for which Java is better suited.

JavaScript

JavaScript is the rare language that the more I use it the more I like it. The only reason node.js has not replaced Python as my goto languages it that Python has good libraries that do what I need, this is less true of JavaScript.

R

I had started to play with R in 2007, but in the last five years have been using it quite a bit for several of the MOOCs I have been taking.

MatLab / Octave

I used Matlab for exploratory data analysis at Geo-Centers and G.F. Goodrich Aerospace. More recently I have used both Matlab and Octave, a GNU clone, for several MOOCs.

Scala

I had played a little with Scala before I took Coursera's Functional Programming Principles in Scala with Martin Odersky, the designer of the language. I really like it, but have not done a lot with it yet.

Lisp

I have used Lisp off an on since the early 1980's. I have used many different dialects including Scheme, Common Lisp, and Clojure but recently I have mostly been using Racket.

Ruby

I have used Ruby for several MOOCs. I like it, even though it follows Perl's, "There is more than one way to do it," philosophy, and I prefer Pythons, "there is one obvious way to do it." Unfortunately I have not found good math and science libraries for Ruby.

Prolog

I used Prolog at Ceder Systems and at Information Engineering Systems. It is not so much a language as a way of thinking about the problem.

J

While I have dabbled with J for many years, until recently I have not taken the deep dive it deserves. Now I am diving deep because I am using it on my Android.

Other Languages

Assembler was my first programming language. I have used many other languages including Pascal, PL/1, BASIC, FORTRAN, COBOL, Haskell, ML, Oz, Php, Icon, Ada, Mathmatica, Forth, and Rexx.
